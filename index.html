MISSION 🌱

Every minute, one million plastic bottles are purchased worldwide. 91% will never be recycled. Cortex intercepts that waste stream — shreds, extrudes, and 3D-prints it into something beautiful, functional, and rooted.

THE ORIGIN
Harris Wu & Joshua Sat · Long Island, NY

As engineers, we looked into design and aesthetics for the household.

Through time and effort, we manufactured the idea. Idea after idea — sketched, modeled, reprinted, discarded — we slowly created the product and refined it to what it is now.

The earliest version of Cortex was a single hex cell with a flat lid. It leaked. The second had a drip tube in the wrong place. The third cracked at the first layer line. The version you see on this page is the result of dozens of iterations, a drawer of failed prints, and a conviction that sustainability shouldn't look like compromise.

This is what upcycling looks like when engineers design it — not as a gesture, but as a product.

Meet rPETG.

Recycled polyethylene terephthalate glycol. The strongest, most food-safe thermoplastic a desktop 3D printer can reliably process — and the one we make from waste bottles.

Why PETG over PET? Glycol modification keeps the polymer amorphous — preventing in-nozzle crystallization, reducing brittleness, and making it 3D-printable at scale. Same source material. Better mechanics.

03 / PRODUCT

The Cortex hanging planter.

A 5-hex base cluster, a stepped hex riser, a removable reservoir cube, and a hollow ring at the top that accepts any hook. Designed to sit on a shelf or hang in a window — and to look intentional from every angle.

RENDER · CORTEX CLUSTER · 5-CELL MODULE WITH REMOVABLE RESERVOIR SCALE 1:3
⬡

5-hex base cluster

Four plant cells radiate from one central chamber. The tessellating geometry is printed as a single rigid piece — no joints, no glue, no failure points.

◼

Removable reservoir

The cube-shaped water reservoir lifts straight off the top. Refill it at the sink, drop it back on. No tubes to disconnect, no drips, no tools.

◯

Hollow hook ring

A clean circular opening at the top of the column is printed hollow — slip any standard 3/8" hook, S-ring, or eye screw through it and you're hanging.

↓

Gravity-fed irrigation

Water flows from the reservoir down through the central column, into the hex riser, and distributes outward to each of the four plant cells — all passive, no pump.

🔩

Sealed metal base

The bottom is capped with 304 stainless steel, silicone-gasketed against the printed shell. Zero leakage onto your shelf, your desk, or your floor.

♻️

100% upcycled polymer

Every shell is printed from post-consumer PET water bottles — shredded, extruded, and spooled in-house. Twelve bottles per unit. Batch ID etched on the base.

05 / SUPPLY CHAIN

How we source the waste.

No family bins. No neighborhood drop-offs. A three-channel collection strategy that scales past the pilot and into the enterprise — inspired by TerraCycle's B2B model.

01

School & district partnerships

Partnering with 8 Nassau County public schools (Bellmore-Merrick, Wantagh, Massapequa districts) to install branded Cortex collection bins in cafeterias. Districts receive quarterly impact reports for their sustainability scorecards; we receive a sorted, clean stream of 500–1,200 bottles per week per school.

~7,200 bottles / week (8 schools)
02

Corporate office take-back

Office buildings already run recycling — but PET bottles get mixed with glass, metal, and contaminated containers, which downgrades the bale. Cortex offers free pickup of segregated clear-PET-only bins from Class-A office buildings in Manhattan, Long Island City, and Brooklyn. In exchange, tenants receive a branded "Zero-to-Landfill" certification and a planter discount.

~18,000 bottles / week (25 offices)
03

Municipal MRF buy-side contract

We contract directly with Nassau County's Materials Recovery Facility in Westbury to buy sorted, baled clear-PET #1 at wholesale — $0.18 per pound. This is the large-volume backbone; schools and offices are the brand-story front end.
